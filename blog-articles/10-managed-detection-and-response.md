## Introduction

Every organization needs around-the-clock security monitoring. Attackers do not keep business hours — breaches are just as likely to start at 3 a.m. on a Sunday as they are mid-afternoon on a Tuesday. But building an in-house Security Operations Center (SOC) capable of genuine 24/7 threat detection and response requires a minimum of 8–12 full-time analysts, significant technology investment, and continuous training to keep pace with evolving threats.

The cost: $2–5 million annually for a competent in-house SOC. The reality for most organizations: they settle for a skeleton crew during business hours and leave the nights and weekends covered by automated alerts with no human to act on them.

Managed Detection and Response (MDR) closes that gap — delivering expert 24/7 threat monitoring, detection, and response at a fraction of the in-house cost.

## What Is MDR?

Managed Detection and Response is a security service in which an external provider continuously monitors a client's environment, detects threats using advanced analytics and threat intelligence, and actively responds to contain and remediate incidents.

MDR is distinct from older managed security services in a critical way: it is not just monitoring and alerting. MDR providers take action on confirmed threats — isolating endpoints, blocking connections, terminating processes, and containing incidents — with or without waiting for client approval on pre-authorized response actions.

Core MDR capabilities include:

- **24/7 threat monitoring** across endpoint, network, cloud, and identity layers
- **Advanced threat detection** using AI/ML and human threat hunting
- **Guided or managed response** — analysts take containment actions, not just send alerts
- **Threat intelligence integration** keeping detection current with the latest attack techniques
- **Regular threat hunting** — proactive searches for threats that evaded automated detection
- **Reporting and transparency** — detailed incident reports and regular security posture briefings

## MDR vs. Traditional MSSP: What's the Difference?

Many organizations confuse MDR with traditional Managed Security Service Providers (MSSPs). The distinction matters:

| Dimension | Traditional MSSP | MDR |
|-----------|-----------------|-----|
| Primary function | Monitor and alert | Detect, investigate, and respond |
| Response capability | Alerts client, client responds | Active response by provider analysts |
| Detection technology | Rule-based SIEM | AI/ML + human threat hunting |
| Threat hunting | Rare | Regular and structured |
| Analyst expertise | Generalist | Deep incident response expertise |
| Dwell time reduction | Limited | Significant |
| Cost | Lower | Higher (but higher value) |

Traditional MSSPs generate alerts and hand them to the client for action. MDR providers investigate those alerts, determine real threats from false positives, and take action.

## What MDR Covers

### Endpoint Coverage
MDR providers deploy EDR agents across all managed endpoints — laptops, servers, and often cloud workloads. These agents provide continuous behavioral telemetry that MDR analysts use to investigate suspicious activity. When a confirmed threat is detected, analysts can remotely isolate the endpoint, kill processes, and collect forensic artifacts.

### Network Coverage
Network traffic analysis feeds MDR platforms with visibility into communication patterns — detecting command-and-control communications, lateral movement, and data exfiltration attempts. Some MDR providers deploy their own network sensors; others integrate with existing customer infrastructure.

### Cloud Coverage
Modern MDR services extend into cloud environments — monitoring AWS CloudTrail, Azure Activity Logs, and GCP Audit Logs for suspicious activity, unusual API calls, and misconfiguration-driven exposure.

### Identity Coverage
Identity-based attacks (credential theft, business email compromise, account takeover) are now the most common initial access vector. MDR coverage of identity platforms (Azure AD, Okta, Active Directory) catches impossible travel, authentication anomalies, and privilege abuse.

## Evaluating MDR Providers: Key Questions to Ask

**What is your mean time to respond?**
The best MDR providers respond to confirmed critical incidents within 15–30 minutes. Ask for documented SLAs and evidence of historical performance.

**What response actions can you take on our behalf without approval?**
Pre-authorized response is what separates MDR from alert-only services. Understand precisely what actions the provider will take autonomously versus requiring client approval.

**How do you handle threat hunting?**
Ask how often analysts proactively hunt for threats (versus waiting for automated alerts), what data sources they hunt across, and what recent hunts have uncovered for clients in your industry.

**What is your coverage model?**
Verify that 24/7 means actual analysts, not automated responses only. Understand geographic coverage, language support, and how incidents are handed off across shifts.

**How do you integrate with our existing technology?**
Good MDR providers work with your existing tools — SIEM, EDR, cloud security platforms — rather than requiring wholesale replacement of your security stack.

**What reporting do we receive?**
Expect weekly executive summaries, detailed incident reports within 24 hours of any incident, and monthly trend briefings with your assigned security team.

**Do you have experience in our industry?**
Industry-specific threat intelligence and regulatory knowledge (HIPAA for healthcare, PCI for retail/finance) are significant value-adds for regulated industries.

## What MDR Costs — and How to Justify It

MDR pricing varies significantly by scope and provider, but typical ranges:

- **Small organizations (250–500 endpoints):** $50,000–$150,000/year
- **Mid-size organizations (500–2,500 endpoints):** $150,000–$400,000/year
- **Enterprise (2,500+ endpoints):** $400,000–$1,500,000+/year

Compare this against the fully-loaded cost of an in-house SOC ($2–5M/year) and the average cost of a breach ($4.45M per IBM's 2023 Cost of a Data Breach Report), and the ROI equation is clear for most organizations.

## When MDR Is the Right Choice

MDR is particularly well-suited for:

- **Organizations without a mature in-house SOC** who need immediate uplift
- **Mid-size companies** that cannot justify the headcount for 24/7 in-house coverage
- **Companies under rapid growth** needing security capability that scales with the business
- **Regulated industries** requiring compliance evidence of continuous monitoring
- **Organizations after an incident** needing rapid capability enhancement
