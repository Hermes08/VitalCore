LETTER № 01 · FROM THE FOUNDER Why I left medicine to make supplements. POSTED — MARCH 2026

Dear reader,
a confession.

Vesper № 01 MMXXVI

For twelve years I was an internal-medicine doctor in a public hospital. I saw, on average, one new patient every nine minutes. Most of them were not sick in the way medicine is built to fix. They were tired. They didn't sleep. Their joints hurt on Thursdays. Their mothers were slipping. Their blood work came back "normal" and their lives did not feel normal.

I did the only thing the system let me do: I wrote prescriptions, or I didn't. I sent them home with advice that I knew, on the drive back, they would not follow. And I began, slowly, to hate my job.

The thing nobody tells you in medical school is that the boring answers are usually right. Sleep. Walk. Eat fibre. Sit less. Pay attention to what you eat. Go outside. Talk to a friend. Drink water. These will outperform 90% of what I was allowed to prescribe, for 90% of what walked through my door.

This is the whole hidden curriculum of internal medicine, in one paragraph.

"Supplements are the most cynical category in consumer health. I should know — I used to roll my eyes at every patient who brought a shopping bag of them."

And yet — some of them were right. Not the gummies. Not the goji. Not the 19-ingredient adaptogenic cortisol-balancing morning mushroom coffee. But the boring ones: magnesium for an evening nervous system, omega-3 for a thirty-year-old with early arterial stiffening, vitamin D for a woman who worked in an office tower and hadn't seen the sun in March. These things moved numbers on lab reports. I had to admit, grudgingly, that in the small space left after lifestyle, the right molecule at the right dose did something measurable.

But getting the right molecule at the right dose in the supplement aisle was, and is, a disaster. Here's what I found when I started looking, in 2022:

  1. Most multis contain folic acid, the synthetic form, even though 40% of the population has trouble processing it, and the methylated form costs two cents more per bottle.
  2. Most zinc is oxide. Your body absorbs around 10% of it. Bisglycinate is four times better and makes pills slightly more expensive.
  3. "Proprietary blends" exist so companies don't have to tell you how much of anything is in the bottle. A 750mg blend can legally be 740mg of rice flour.
  4. The prettiest brands I knew — the pastel ones, the ones that sponsored podcasts — were the worst offenders on dose and form, consistently. Marketing budget correlates inversely with quality.

So in 2023 I took a sabbatical, which became a resignation. I spent a year talking to formulators, pharmacognosy professors, toxicologists, and — honestly, the best source — independent lab scientists who test what's actually inside supplement bottles for a living. I learned three more things:

Three more things I learned — The good ingredients are not secret. They are boring, and expensive.
— Third-party testing is not glamorous. It is the entire ball game.
— You cannot make a good, affordable multivitamin. You can make a good, fairly-priced one.

This last point mattered. The market was split, savagely, between a $9 grocery-store multi with 40 ingredients at token doses, and a $180 "wellness" multi with 17 ingredients at overdose quantities, usually with a mushroom on the label. There was no place in between for a serious, honest, sufficient formula. And that is what we decided to make.

Vesper Daily N° 01 is a 24-ingredient foundation. It is the formula I wish I could have prescribed. It has:

Active forms only. Methyl B-vitamins. Chelated minerals. A third-party-tested vitamin D dose that is actually enough to move blood levels. Mixed tocopherols, not synthetic. K₂ paired with D₃. No proprietary blends. No gummies. No sugar. No mushroom on the label.

It is not a cure for anything. It is a foundation — the floor — underneath which you can decide, with your clinician or by yourself, what you need to add. On this site you'll find the other eleven formulas we make, most of them quieter than the multi: magnesium, ashwagandha, omega-3, a short list of the best-evidence single-molecule supports. We will not ever add NMN, resveratrol, "methylation boosters," or anything that requires a press release to justify.

When we launched in January 2025, my biggest fear was the third category of email: the one where a reader tries Vesper and writes back to say, "I don't feel anything." This is, incredibly, the most common message we receive — and it is the highest compliment a supplement can receive. Good daily supplements don't feel like anything. Tomorrow's labs look a little better. Next winter, your kids' colds don't become your colds. That's the whole contract.

A good supplement feels like nothing. A bad one feels like marketing.

If you are still with me at this point: thank you. I wrote this because I wanted you to know why a former doctor is in your inbox selling capsules. The short version is that the boring answers are usually right, and I got tired of watching people spend hundreds of dollars a month on the wrong, pretty ones. I made the thing I wish I'd been allowed to recommend.

The long version is that I love this work more than I loved the job I left. Every Monday I open our lab's certificate of analysis and I can tell you, to the microgram, what's in the bottle that shows up on your doorstep. I sleep better. I hope you will, too.

P.S. — I still think about a handful of my old patients. A retired teacher named Teresa. A carpenter named Luis. The barista whose grandmother slipped. If any of you are reading this: I'm sorry I couldn't do more in the nine minutes. I hope this does a little more.
