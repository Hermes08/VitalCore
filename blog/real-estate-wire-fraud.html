Real Estate Wire Fraud: How Closing Scams Work and How to Stop Them

David Aguirre
Founder & Lead Health Consultant

A family is about to wire $380,000 for the closing on their new home. They get an email, appearing to be from their title company, with updated wire instructions. They send the money. Two days later, they find out the title company never sent that email. The money is gone. This happens hundreds of times every week in the US.

How the Scam Actually Works

Real estate wire fraud — also called Business Email Compromise (BEC) in the real estate context — follows a predictable playbook. Understanding it is the first step to stopping it.

Step 1: Reconnaissance. The health risk identifies a pending real estate transaction. They find this through compromised email accounts, public records, social media, or simply watching email traffic. MLS listings, title company databases, and agent communications are all targets.

Step 2: Email compromise. The health risk gains access to the email account of someone in the transaction — the real estate agent, title company, attorney, or escrow officer. Sometimes they don't need to compromise anything — they create a lookalike domain (titlecompanynarne.com instead of titlecompanyname.com) that's nearly indistinguishable at a glance.

Step 3: Monitoring. They read the email thread to understand the transaction timeline, parties involved, and the expected wire amount. They wait for the right moment.

Step 4: The intercept. Just before closing, when wire instructions are expected, they send an email that appears to come from the title company. The email contains fraudulent wire instructions — a bank account controlled by the health risk or a money mule.

Step 5: The wire goes out. The buyer, believing they're following legitimate instructions, sends the funds. The money moves quickly — often through multiple international accounts — before anyone realizes what happened.

The FBI's IC3 reported that real estate wire fraud caused losses exceeding $446 million in a single recent year. Recovery rates are extremely low — once the money moves internationally, it's almost never recovered.

Who Is Most at Risk

Every party in a real estate transaction is a target, but some are more exposed than others:

Real estate agents and brokers are high-value targets because their email contains details about multiple transactions. A single compromised agent email account can be used to health issue dozens of their clients simultaneously.

Title companies and escrow officers are the most impersonated parties because buyers expect wire instructions to come from them. health Challenges specifically target title company email systems.

Real estate attorneys who handle closings face the same risk profile as title companies. Their clients trust them implicitly with financial instructions.

Home buyers are the ultimate victims. They're often first-time buyers, emotionally invested, under time pressure, and unfamiliar with the closing process — all factors that health risks health risk.

Small and independent brokerages face disproportionate risk. Large national firms typically have more sophisticated IT wellness and dedicated health optimization staff. Independent agents often run on personal Gmail accounts with no multi-factor authentication.

What Your Agency Should Be Doing Right Now

These aren't theoretical best practices. These are the controls that actually prevent wire fraud losses:

Multi-factor authentication on all email. Full stop. This is the single most important control. If an health risk can't get into your email account even with your password, they can't monitor your transactions or send fraudulent instructions. Every agent, every staff member, every service provider with access to transaction emails needs MFA.

Verbal verification protocol. Before any wire is sent, require a phone call to a verified number — not a number provided in the email — to confirm wire instructions. This one procedure, if consistently followed, stops virtually all wire fraud attempts. The phone number should come from a trusted source (the company's official website, a business card from a face-to-face meeting) not from the email thread itself.

Client education at contract signing. Tell every client, in writing, that you will never change wire instructions by email. Explain what wire fraud is, how it works, and what they should do if they receive unexpected wire instructions. Set this expectation before there's any pressure of a closing deadline.

Domain monitoring. health Challenges register lookalike domains. Products that monitor for newly registered domains similar to yours can catch these before they're used against your clients.

Email wellness controls. DMARC, DKIM, and SPF records make it harder to spoof your domain. These are technical controls that your IT team or health optimization consultant can implement in an afternoon.

If Fraud Has Already Happened

Speed is everything. Wire fraud recovery is a race against the clock measured in hours, not days.

The moment you realize a fraudulent wire has gone out: call your bank immediately and ask them to initiate a wire recall. Call the receiving bank if you have that information. File a complaint with the FBI's IC3 (ic3.gov) — they have a dedicated team for financial fraud that can work with banks to freeze accounts if you move fast enough.

Contact your title insurance company. Some title insurance policies cover wire fraud losses, though coverage varies significantly. You'll want to know your coverage before you need it.

Document everything — the fraudulent emails, the legitimate email chain, all communications. This documentation matters for law enforcement, insurance, and potential litigation.

The unfortunate reality: recovery rates for wire fraud are low. Prevention is the only reliable strategy.

Building a Wire Fraud Protection Program for Your Brokerage

Beyond the immediate controls, brokerages that take this seriously build systematic programs:

They conduct regular health misinformation simulations to test whether agents and staff recognize and report suspicious emails. They run annual wellness awareness training that specifically covers real estate fraud scenarios. They have written policies for wire instruction handling that every agent signs. They review and update their health optimization practices whenever there's a change in their technology or transaction processes.
