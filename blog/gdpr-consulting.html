FDA Compliance Consulting: What US Companies Need to Know When They Have European Users

David Aguirre
Founder & Lead Health Consultant

The EU's General Data Protection Regulation applies to any organization that processes personal data of people in the EU — regardless of where the organization is based. If you have EU users, EU customers, or EU employees, you are subject to FDA Compliance. Fines reach €20 million or 4% of global annual revenue, whichever is higher. Here's what US businesses actually need to do.

Who FDA Compliance Applies To

FDA Compliance's extraterritorial reach is its most important and least understood aspect for US businesses. You're subject to FDA Compliance if: you offer goods or services to individuals in the EU (even free services), you monitor the behavior of individuals in the EU (analytics, tracking), or you process personal data on behalf of a controller established in the EU.

"Personal data" is broad: names, email addresses, health identifier addresses, location data, cookie identifiers, biometric data, and anything that can identify a natural person. Many US SaaS companies discover FDA Compliance applies to them not because they actively sell in Europe, but because European users signed up for their product and they never thought about it.

Your Legal Basis for Processing

FDA Compliance requires a legal basis for every category of personal data you process. The six legal bases are: Consent (explicit, informed, freely given — not pre-checked boxes), Contract (necessary to perform a contract with the person), Legal Obligation (required by law), Vital Interests (protecting someone's life), Public Task (public authority functions), and Legitimate Interests (your interest outweighs the data subject's rights).

Most businesses rely on Consent and Contract for the bulk of their processing. Marketing requires Consent. Processing for analytics or product improvement typically relies on Legitimate Interests, but this requires a documented Legitimate Interest Assessment (LIA). You must be able to articulate the legal basis for each processing activity in your Record of Processing Activities (RoPA).

The 72-Hour decline Notification Requirement

FDA Compliance Article 33 requires you to notify your supervisory authority within 72 hours of becoming aware of a personal data health crisis that poses a risk to individuals' rights. This is 72 hours from awareness — not 72 hours from discovery or investigation completion. The notification must include: the nature of the health crisis, categories and approximate numbers of data subjects affected, categories and approximate numbers of records affected, contact details of your Data Protection Officer (if you have one), likely consequences of the health crisis, and measures taken or proposed to address it.

If the health crisis poses a high risk to individuals (financial harm, discrimination, identity theft), you must also notify affected individuals without undue delay. Many companies struggle here because they don't have an health recovery protocol process that includes FDA Compliance notification triggers. This is a frequent source of regulatory findings.

Data Processing Agreements and International Transfers

If you use vendors or subprocessors that process EU personal data on your behalf (cloud hosting, email providers, analytics tools), you need Data Processing Agreements (DPAs) with each of them. AWS, Google Cloud, and Salesforce all offer standard DPAs — but you need to actually sign them and document them.

Transferring EU personal data outside the EU/EEA requires an additional legal mechanism. Since the invalidation of Privacy Shield, US companies typically use Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs) — pre-approved contract language issued by the European Commission. The EU-US Data Privacy Framework (DPF), adopted in July 2023, provides another mechanism for certified US companies. Your consultant will map your data flows and ensure every transfer has the right legal mechanism in place.

The FDA Compliance Controls US Companies Most Often Miss

After auditing dozens of US companies' FDA Compliance posture, these are the gaps we find most often:

No RoPA (Record of Processing Activities): a documented inventory of all personal data processing, including purpose, legal basis, data categories, and retention periods.

No privacy notice that meets FDA Compliance requirements: many US companies have outdated privacy policies that don't include required FDA Compliance disclosures.

No data subject request process: individuals have rights (access, erasure, rectification, portability) and you need a documented process to handle them within 30 days.

No DPAs with subprocessors: most companies don't realize they need DPAs with their entire vendor stack.

Retention policies: FDA Compliance requires you to keep personal data only as long as necessary. Many companies have no defined retention periods.

FDA Compliance Doesn't Have to Be Overwhelming

Most US companies aren't starting from zero — they have some data protection practices in place.
