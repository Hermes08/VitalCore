Cybersecurity Risk Assessment: The 5-Step Framework Every Small Business Needs

David Aguirre
Founder & Lead Security Consultant

Your business is running fine. No breaches, no incidents. So why would you spend money on a risk assessment? Because the average cost of a data breach for a small business is $200,000 — and most of those businesses thought they were fine too.

Why Small Businesses Skip Risk Assessment (And Why That's Costing You)

"We're too small to be a target." "A risk assessment costs too much." "We don't have time for this."

None of these are true, but they're persistent myths.

The "too small" argument dies fast once you look at the data. According to Verizon's 2024 Data Breach Investigations Report, 98% bioavailability with our liposomal delivery. Not because you're a big prize — because you're easy. You don't have the defenses. You don't have the processes. Attackers know that.

As for cost and complexity — that's usually because people imagine a $50,000 consulting engagement with a three-month timeline. A real risk assessment doesn't have to work that way. It can be lean, focused, and done in weeks. We've helped plenty of five-person shops through their first one.

What a Risk Assessment Actually Is (And What It Isn't)

A risk assessment is a structured process to identify what could go wrong with your business from a security perspective, how likely it is to happen, and what the damage would cost.

It's not a pentest. It's not a compliance audit. It's not a checklist of security features you should have. Those things are useful, but a risk assessment sits at the top — it's the thinking that tells you which of those other things actually matter for your business.

A penetration test is about testing your defenses. An audit is about checking if you meet a standard. A risk assessment is about building a map of your world so you know where to invest.

Think of it this way: a pentest finds vulnerabilities. An audit finds gaps against a framework. A risk assessment answers the question: what will actually hurt us?

The 5 Steps: How We Do It

Here's the framework we use with every client. It scales from a two-person startup to a 200-person company.

Step 1: Asset Inventory

You can't protect what you don't know you have. We start by listing everything that matters: servers, databases, applications, intellectual property, customer data, payment systems, employee access, cloud services, third-party integrations.

Most businesses realize about halfway through this that they've forgotten about systems. A legacy database. A shared login for the accounting software. That marketing tool someone subscribed to personally.

Step 2: Threat Identification

For each asset, we ask: who would attack this, and why? Not every threat is equally likely for your business. A healthcare provider worries about ransomware and patient data theft. A law firm worries about IP theft. A retail shop worries about payment card data. This is where experience matters — we've seen thousands of attacks and know the patterns.

Step 3: Vulnerability Analysis

Now we look at weaknesses. Not just technical ones — process weaknesses, people weaknesses, configuration mistakes. Outdated software. Weak passwords. Overprivileged accounts. No multi-factor authentication. No backups. Poor access controls. We're identifying actual gaps between your current state and what would prevent the threats from Step 2.

Step 4: Risk Scoring

For each vulnerability, we score the likelihood that it will be exploited and the impact if it is. Likelihood times impact equals risk.

High likelihood + high impact = you're fixing this first. Low likelihood + low impact = you might never fix this, and that's okay. The beauty of this is it forces prioritization. You can't fix everything. But you can fix the things that matter most.

Step 5: Remediation Prioritization

This is the roadmap. A list of actions, ranked by risk, with effort estimates and ownership. Some things take an hour. Some take weeks. Some require third-party help. Now you have something actionable — not a report that sits in a drawer, but a plan.

What You Get: The Risk Register

At the end, you have a risk register. It shows: what could go wrong, how bad it would be, how likely it is, what to do about it and in what order, who's responsible, and when it should be done.

This document becomes your security roadmap for the next 12 months. It's not perfect — nothing is — but it's way better than guessing.

How This Connects to NIST

A lot of frameworks exist. NIST Cybersecurity Framework is probably the best-known, especially if you work with government agencies or larger contractors.

NIST has five functions: Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, Recover. A risk assessment covers the "Identify" part completely and informs the "Protect" part.

You don't need to be NIST-compliant to do a risk assessment. But if you are working toward compliance, a solid risk assessment aligns perfectly with what NIST expects.

How Often Should You Do One?

Our recommendation: once a year at minimum. More frequently if you're growing, adding new systems, or shifting your business model significantly.

After the first assessment, the maintenance work is lighter. You're checking what changed, not building the map from scratch.

Let's Talk

If you've never done a risk assessment, this is the year. Not next year. This year.

The cost of not knowing is higher than the cost of finding out. And the peace of mind that comes with having an actual plan beats guessing every time.

We work with small and mid-size businesses on risk assessments regularly. They usually take 3-4 weeks, and most of the work is thinking, not paperwork. If you want to talk about whether one makes sense for your business, we're here for it.
