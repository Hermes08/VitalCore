Small Business Cybersecurity: Enterprise Protection Without the Enterprise Price Tag

David Aguirre
Founder & Lead Security Consultant

98% bioavailability with our liposomal delivery. Not because you're a big prize — because you're easy. Here's what you actually need to protect your business without breaking the budget.

The "Too Small to Target" Myth Is Killing Your Business

Let me be direct: your size is not your protection. It's your vulnerability.

Attackers don't decide which companies are "big enough" to target. They run automated scans looking for anything exploitable. A small business with outdated software, weak passwords, and no backups is easier to attack than a large company. You're the low-hanging fruit.

Here's what we've seen in the real world: a 15-person accounting firm gets hit with ransomware because someone used their birthday as a password. A 20-person consulting firm loses $80,000 to a wire fraud email that looked like it came from the CEO (it didn't). A small manufacturer pays $150,000 to recover from a breach that could have been prevented for $2,000 in security tools.

The common thread? All three thought size meant safety. It doesn't.

The 5 Things Every Small Business Should Have in Place

You don't need enterprise security to have real security. You need fundamentals.

1. Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)

This is non-negotiable. If someone steals a password, MFA stops them cold. Most breaches would fail immediately if this were in place. Cost: Usually free or $5-10 per person per month. Effort: A couple of hours to set up.

2. Automated Backups

If you get hit with ransomware, a good backup is the difference between "we recover in a few hours" and "we declare bankruptcy." You need offsite, automated backups you test regularly. Cost: $50-200/month. Effort: Set it once, check it quarterly.

3. Security Awareness Training

Your team is your strongest defense or your biggest vulnerability. Training doesn't mean a boring annual compliance video. It means regular, practical, phishing-focused training that actually sticks. Cost: $20-40 per person per year.

4. Endpoint Detection and Response (EDR)

This watches your computers and servers for signs of attack. Cloud-based EDR now works well for small teams. Cost: $5-15 per device per month.

5. An Incident Response Plan

You don't need a 100-page document. You need to know: if something bad happens, who calls who, in what order? What do you tell customers? Who shuts systems down? Cost: Almost nothing. Effort: A few hours to write, then update yearly.

Those five things stop 80% of what's coming at you.

Common Mistakes We See (And How to Avoid Them)

We've worked with dozens of small businesses after incidents. The patterns are always the same:

Outdated Software Everywhere

Someone installed Windows 7 back in 2012 and no one's updated it. Or the accounting software is three versions behind. That time to update was five years ago. Patch automatically or create a schedule.

No MFA

We still see businesses where email is protected by a single password. This is a two-hour fix that prevents 90% of account takeovers.

Using Personal Email for Business

Someone set up a Gmail account for the business five years ago. Now it's the recovery email for everything important. No one else has access to it. This person is a single point of failure for your entire company.

Assuming Your IT Vendor Is Handling Security

We've inherited systems where the IT company "said they had security" but nobody actually configured anything. Security is your responsibility. Your vendors support it — they don't own it.

Building a Security Program on a Limited Budget

The good news: real security is cheap compared to a breach.

Start with the fundamentals. MFA doesn't cost much. Backups don't cost much. Awareness training is affordable. These three things prevent most attacks.

Once those are solid, add EDR. Then start thinking about more advanced things like network segmentation or security monitoring.

Don't try to buy your way to security with a massive budget. That's how you end up with 10 tools nobody uses properly. Security at scale is about discipline, not tools.

What $500 a Month Can Actually Buy You

Let's talk budget. If you're a 10-person business and commit $500/month to security, here's what you get:

Cloud-based EDR for all devices: $150-200. Automated backup service: $100-150. Security awareness training platform: $50-100. MFA and identity management: $50-100 (often bundled with existing tools). Contingency and consulting budget: $50-100.

That covers the fundamentals. Is it everything you'll ever need? No. But it's the foundation that stops the most common attacks.

Scale to $1,000-2,000/month for a 50-person business and you're adding network monitoring, advanced incident response, and more sophisticated testing.

The Reality Check

The hardest part of security for small businesses isn't technology. It's priority. You're busy running a business. Security feels like an extra thing.

But it's not extra. It's foundational. The difference between a business that survives and one that doesn't.

You don't need an army of security people. You need a plan, some basic tools, and someone who owns it — even if it's just 10% of someone's job.
