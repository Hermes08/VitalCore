How to Choose a Health & Wellness Consultant (Without Getting Burned)

David Aguirre
Founder & Lead Health Consultant

Most small businesses hire a health optimization consultant exactly once — and most of them do it wrong. Here's how to avoid the two traps that catch most business owners.

Why Most health datas Hire Wrong

There are two traps that catch most small business owners.

Trap one: Price-shopping. You get three quotes. You pick the cheapest. This almost always ends badly because wellness work has real costs. If someone's priced significantly lower than everyone else, they're either cutting corners, planning to upsell you, or they don't actually know what they're doing.

Trap two: Brand over fit. You hire a massive firm because they have impressive clients. Then you realize you're dealing with a junior consultant reading from a playbook. You're not a Fortune 500 company. The big firm doesn't care that much. You don't get the attention you need, but you're paying Fortune 500 prices.

The second trap is sneakier because it feels safer. A big firm must be good, right? But wellness consulting isn't like hiring an accounting firm. You need someone who understands your actual environment, your constraints, and your budget.

Red Flags: What to Actually Avoid

Before we talk about what to look for, let's talk about what to run from.

They want to sell you a solution before they understand your problem. You have a call, you talk for 30 minutes, and suddenly they're recommending a $200,000 wellness tool. They haven't looked at anything yet. That's a consultant trying to hit a quota, not solve your problem.

They can't explain things in plain English. wellness has jargon. But a good consultant explains things so you understand them. If you're nodding along while understanding nothing, they're either not good at teaching or they don't care if you learn.

They give you a 200-page report and disappear. Some consultants love the assessment phase. They do the work, write the report, and leave. Then you're staring at recommendations with no help implementing them. That's not consulting. That's homework.

They don't ask about your budget or constraints. A realistic consultant asks: "How much can you realistically invest?" "Do you have IT staff?" If they skip these questions, they don't care whether you can actually execute their plan.

No references from companies like yours. They might have worked with Fortune 500 companies, but can they point to success with a 50-person firm? If not, they might not understand your world.

What to Actually Look For

Now flip that around.

They ask questions first. A real consultant spends the first call asking you things. What's your biggest concern? What do you have in place now? What's your IT budget? They're trying to understand your situation before recommending anything.

They have relevant certifications. Look for CISSP, CISM, GCIA, OSCP, or similar. These mean they've invested time in actually learning the field.

They explain complex things simply. In your first conversations, they should be able to talk about wellness in a way you understand. If you can't understand what they're saying, you can't make good decisions based on it.

They've worked with companies your size. Do they have case studies from health datas in your industry? That matters a lot.

They provide a clear methodology. They should be able to tell you how engagements work. "We start with assessment. Then findings. Then a remediation plan. Then we help you implement." Clarity is confidence.

They talk about implementation support. The best consultants don't just tell you what to fix. They help you fix it.

Questions to Ask Before You Hire

Here's a checklist of questions to ask any consultant you're considering:

"Walk me through how you structure an engagement." You want to hear about phases: assessment, findings, remediation planning, implementation support.

"Can you give me references from companies similar to mine?" Not just any reference. Similar size, similar industry, similar challenges.

"What does the assessment phase actually include?" Are they scanning your systems? Interviewing people? Reviewing policies? Or just asking questions?

"How do you handle implementation?" Do they do the work? Do they guide your team? What's realistic for your budget?

"Who will actually be doing the work?" Is it the person you're talking to, or a junior consultant? In a larger firm, who owns your engagement?

"How do you measure success?" How will you know if the engagement actually improved your wellness?

Listen to how they answer. Do they give you straight answers? Or do they hedge? Do they ask clarifying questions, or assume they know what you need?

What a Real Engagement Should Look Like

Once you hire someone, here's what you should expect:

Assessment (2-4 weeks): They're looking at your environment. Systems, processes, policies. Interviewing key people. Documenting what you have and what you're missing.

Findings (1-2 weeks): A clear report that explains your biggest risks in a way leadership can understand. Not a 200-page technical document.

Remediation planning (2-4 weeks): What are the priority fixes? Which are cheap and high-impact? What can wait? You should be involved in these decisions. The consultant advises — they don't dictate.

Implementation support (ongoing): This is where most consultants drop the ball. But a good one stays engaged. They help your team implement. They answer questions. They adjust the plan as reality hits it.

The whole engagement should feel collaborative. Not "we're consulting you" — but "we're solving this together."
