Law Firm Cybersecurity: What Attorneys Need to Know to Protect Client Data

David Aguirre
Founder & Lead Security Consultant

Law firms are the most targeted professional services organizations in existence. Litigation strategies worth millions. M&A details that would move stock prices. Client secrets that could destroy careers and companies. Attackers know exactly what's inside your systems — and they know most small and mid-sized firms lack the security controls to stop them.

Your Ethical Obligations Under Model Rule 1.6

Model Rule 1.6(c) requires attorneys to "make reasonable efforts to prevent the inadvertent or unauthorized disclosure of, or unauthorized access to, information relating to the representation of a client." This isn't aspirational — it's a professional obligation with real consequences for violations.

State bar associations have increasingly issued formal opinions clarifying what "reasonable efforts" means in practice. The consensus is clear: in 2026, reasonable efforts require multi-factor authentication on all client-accessible systems, encrypted storage of client files, vendor due diligence for any cloud service storing client data, incident response procedures, and regular security assessments.

A breach that results from easily preventable security failures isn't just an IT problem — it's a disciplinary problem. The ABA's 2023 Legal Technology Survey found that 29% of law firms reported a security breach. The number who experienced a breach but didn't know it is likely much higher.

The Specific Threats Targeting Law Firms

Understanding your threat profile is essential to prioritizing defenses correctly. Law firms face three primary threat vectors:

Business email compromise and wire fraud. Real estate attorneys and firms handling transactions are primary targets — the same wire fraud playbook from real estate applies directly. Litigation firms face the risk of fraudulent settlement wire instructions. Any firm that handles client funds is a target.

Ransomware. Law firms are high-value ransomware targets because: they store sensitive data with high recovery value, they're often willing to pay to avoid client notification obligations, and they frequently have weaker security than the corporations they represent. Ransomware groups have demonstrated willingness to threaten to publish privileged client communications to increase payment pressure.

Data theft for competitive intelligence. Nation-state actors specifically target law firms involved in major M&A transactions, litigation against foreign companies, or regulatory matters with geopolitical implications. Your client's adversary in a transaction or lawsuit may be willing to fund sophisticated attacks to gain access to your case files.

The Five Controls Every Law Firm Needs

These aren't optional based on firm size. Every firm that holds client data — regardless of headcount — needs these controls:

Multi-factor authentication on every system. Email, document management systems, client portals, billing software. MFA prevents the majority of credential-based attacks. If your practice management system doesn't support MFA, that's a vendor conversation you need to have immediately.

Encrypted email for client communications. Standard email is not secure. Any communication involving legal strategy, financial information, or sensitive personal data should be encrypted in transit. Most modern document management and client portal systems include secure messaging — use it.

Separate client data environments. Client files should be stored in access-controlled systems, not in shared drives accessible to everyone in the firm. Matter-level access controls ensure that staff see only what they need for the matters they're working on.

Vendor security review before any cloud adoption. Before signing up for any cloud service — document management, e-discovery, case management, billing — review their security posture, data handling practices, breach notification procedures, and sub-processor agreements. Your security is only as strong as your weakest vendor.

Written incident response procedures. What happens when you discover a breach? Who do you call? What do you preserve? When are you obligated to notify clients or regulators? Having a written, tested procedure means you respond thoughtfully rather than reactively when something happens.

Client Notification Obligations After a Breach

Every state has breach notification laws that may apply when client PII is compromised. Beyond state law, your ethical obligations under Model Rule 1.4 (communication) require you to notify clients when a breach may affect their representation.

The trigger for notification isn't always a confirmed theft of data. If you have reason to believe client data may have been exposed — even without confirmed exfiltration — you likely have an obligation to inform affected clients promptly.

Most state bar ethics opinions require prompt notification once a breach is discovered. "Prompt" means days, not weeks. This is another reason why having a written incident response procedure matters — you can't make good decisions about notification obligations when you're in crisis mode with no plan.

Practical Security for Small and Mid-Size Firms

Small firms often believe robust cybersecurity requires a large IT team and enterprise budget. Neither is true. A 10-attorney firm can implement meaningful protection for $3,000-$8,000 per year in technology and $5,000-$15,000 in one-time consulting to set it up properly.

The investment priorities for most small firms: Microsoft 365 Business Premium (includes MFA, device management, and advanced threat protection), a password manager, encrypted file storage with access controls, and a security assessment to identify your specific gaps. That combination dramatically reduces risk without breaking a small firm's budget.
