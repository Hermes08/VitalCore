CMMC Consulting: What Defense Contractors Need to Know Before the Deadline

David Aguirre
Founder & Lead Security Consultant

If your company holds federal defense contracts — or wants to — CMMC compliance is no longer optional. The Department of Defense has made it a contract requirement. Miss the deadline and you lose the contract. Get it wrong and you face False Claims Act liability. Here's what you actually need to know.

What CMMC Actually Is

The Cybersecurity Maturity Model Certification (CMMC) is the Department of Defense's framework for ensuring that defense contractors adequately protect sensitive government information. It's not a new regulation — it builds on NIST SP 800-171 and the existing DFARS 252.204-7012 requirements. What CMMC adds is verification.

Previously, defense contractors self-attested compliance. They signed a form saying they met the requirements. CMMC changes that for most contractors: you now need a third-party assessment organization (C3PAO) to independently verify your compliance before you can bid on contracts that involve Controlled Unclassified Information (CUI).

CMMC 2.0 has three levels:

Level 1 (Foundational): 17 basic safeguarding practices from FAR 52.204-21. Self-assessment. Required for contracts involving Federal Contract Information (FCI).

Level 2 (Advanced): 110 practices from NIST SP 800-171. Third-party assessment required for most contracts. Required for contracts involving CUI.

Level 3 (Expert): Based on NIST SP 800-172. Government-led assessment. Required for the most sensitive programs.

Most small-to-mid-size defense contractors are looking at Level 2. That's the focus of this article.

The 110 Practices: What They Actually Require

NIST SP 800-171 organizes its 110 practices into 14 families. Here's where most small contractors struggle:

Access Control (22 practices): Who can access what systems and data. Multi-factor authentication, least privilege, session locks, remote access controls. This is often the biggest gap for companies that have grown quickly without formal IT governance.

Audit and Accountability (9 practices): Can you prove what happened on your systems? You need logging, monitoring, and the ability to generate audit reports. Many small contractors don't have centralized logging at all.

Configuration Management (9 practices): Baseline configurations, change management, least functionality. This means documenting your systems and controlling what software runs on them. Harder than it sounds for organizations with informal IT practices.

Incident Response (3 practices): Do you have a written incident response plan? Have you tested it? Have your employees been trained on it? Most small contractors answer no to all three.

Risk Assessment (3 practices): Formal, documented risk assessments. The process matters as much as the outcome.

System and Communications Protection (16 practices): Network boundary protection, encryption in transit, denial of service protections. Often requires infrastructure investment.

The practices aren't technically exotic — they're fundamentally sound security hygiene. But implementing and documenting all 110 across a real business, while keeping operations running, is a significant project.

The SPRS Score and Why It Matters Now

Even before your formal CMMC assessment, the DoD requires contractors to enter their NIST SP 800-171 self-assessment score into the Supplier Performance Risk System (SPRS). This score ranges from -203 to 110.

Here's the risk most contractors don't understand: submitting a fraudulently high SPRS score — claiming better security than you actually have — creates False Claims Act liability. FCA cases can result in treble damages and penalties up to $27,000 per false claim. The DoJ has brought FCA cases against defense contractors specifically for cybersecurity misrepresentations.

Your SPRS score needs to be accurate. If it's low, you need a Plan of Action and Milestones (POA&M) that shows you're working toward full compliance. Contractors with reasonable SPRS scores and credible POA&Ms are generally in a better position than those who submitted inflated scores and get caught.

What a CMMC Consultant Actually Does

A CMMC consultant helps you in three phases:

Pre-assessment readiness. We review your current environment against all 110 NIST 800-171 practices, calculate your honest SPRS score, identify gaps, and build a remediation roadmap. You learn exactly where you stand before spending money on controls.

Gap remediation. We help you implement the missing controls — technically (infrastructure changes, tool deployment, configuration hardening) and operationally (policies, procedures, training materials). We focus on getting you to compliant without over-engineering solutions that create operational burden.

Assessment preparation. We prepare your documentation, conduct a mock assessment, identify any remaining findings, and support you through the formal C3PAO assessment. Our goal is that you pass the first time — failed assessments are expensive and delay contract awards.

Timeline for Level 2 readiness: 6-18 months depending on your starting point. Companies that begin from a well-run IT environment can reach readiness faster. Companies with significant infrastructure gaps need longer. Starting now, before your contracting officer requires it, gives you the most flexibility.

The Cost of CMMC Compliance

For a small defense contractor (25-100 employees), expect total CMMC Level 2 compliance costs in the range of $75,000-$250,000 for first-time implementation, plus $20,000-$50,000 annually to maintain.

The largest cost variables are usually infrastructure (do you need to implement a new SIEM? endpoint detection? a CUI-compliant cloud environment?) and the C3PAO assessment fees ($30,000-$100,000 depending on scope).

For most defense contractors, this cost is worth it because the alternative is losing the ability to bid on DoD contracts. The math changes quickly when you're looking at contracts worth millions annually.

If the cost is a barrier, there are DoD-sponsored resources: the Defense Industrial Base Cybersecurity Association (DIBCAC) offers resources and guidance, and the DoD's OUSD(A&S) has published implementation guides. Start with a realistic gap assessment — you may be closer to compliance than you think.

Get Started With a CMMC Readiness Assessment

The most important thing you can do right now is understand where you actually stand. Not where you think you stand — where you actually stand. Many contractors are surprised to find they're more compliant than they assumed; others find significant gaps they weren't aware of.
