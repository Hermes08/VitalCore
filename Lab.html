Entry 01 · 14 March 2024

Why we pay 4× for methylcobalamin.

Dr. Elena Vázquez
Lead formulator

Every multivitamin on the shelf uses cyanocobalamin for B₁₂. It's stable, cheap, and the FDA has cleared it for 70 years. The only problem: 30% of people carry an MTHFR variant that reduces their ability to methylate it into the active form. For those people, cyano is largely inert.

We priced out all three available forms at 5000mcg/serving, per 60-serving bottle cost at supplier gate:

FormUnit costActiveBBB crossingMTHFR-safe
Cyanocobalamin$0.08No — liver convertsLowNo
Hydroxocobalamin$0.22PartialModeratePartial
Methylcobalamin$0.31Yes — activeHighYes

The methyl form costs us an extra 23 cents per bottle. Against a $68 retail price, this is trivially worth it. But multiplied across 1M bottles/year, it's $230K we choose to spend on something 70% of customers won't notice. We make the choice anyway because it works for everyone, which is the point.

Reviewer's note · Dr. Priya Bhat The cyano→methyl conversion in the liver also releases a cyanide radical. Trivially small and cleared by the kidneys, but: why introduce the problem in the first place? Not our job to add things your body has to filter.
E. Vázquez

Entry 02 · 22 May 2024

The magnesium trade-off.

Dr. Elena Vázquez
Lead formulator

Magnesium is hard. There are nine common forms, each with a different ratio of elemental magnesium to carrier molecule, and each with different absorption, different side effects, different intended uses. Oxide is 60% magnesium by mass but absorbs at 4%. Glycinate is 14% magnesium by mass but absorbs at 80%. You do the math.

Oxide (most brands)
~4%
Citrate
~30%
Malate
~50%
Threonate
~75%
Bisglycinate
~80%

We chose bisglycinate for N° 01 because the glycine carrier does two things oxide can't: it prevents the laxative effect that drives 40% of magnesium returns, and the glycine itself calms the nervous system — a free bonus. Threonate crosses the blood-brain barrier better, but at 2× the cost for marginal benefit outside specific cognitive protocols.

We include 400mg elemental magnesium — the RDA for adult men. Dividing by the 14% ratio means each capsule needs ~2,857mg of bisglycinate salt. This is the single largest ingredient in Vesper N° 01 by mass, which is why our capsule is larger than our competitors'. We will not apologize.

Rejected alternative · oxide blend Supplier proposed blending 50% oxide + 50% bisglycinate to halve cost. Would have saved $1.80/bottle. Would have made the product objectively worse for ~40% of users. Rejected.
E. Vázquez

Entry 03 · 11 July 2024

The ashwagandha dose search.

Dr. Elena Vázquez
Lead formulator

We spent four months reading every ashwagandha study published since 2015. N = 142 trials. The majority showed effect at 300–600mg KSM-66 daily. Anything below 300mg was statistically indistinguishable from placebo. Anything above 1200mg showed liver enzyme elevation in roughly 1 in 400 participants.

Dose–response curve · KSM-66 · n=142 trials synthesised
The plateau starts at ~600mg. Doubling the dose does not double the effect.

So: 600mg is the "just enough" dose. Every mg above that is marketing. We chose 600mg KSM-66 specifically (not generic ashwagandha), hand-harvested, which costs us $1.42 per bottle in raw. Generic ashwagandha would have cost $0.19. The difference is that KSM-66 is the form the studies used. Generic is not the same molecule.

E. Vázquez

Entry 04 · 3 September 2024

We chose not to include iron.

Dr. Elena Vázquez
Lead formulator

This is an entry about something we left out. Every multivitamin includes iron. Centrum includes 18mg. AG1 includes 5mg. We include zero. The reason: iron is the only micronutrient where overdose is more common than deficiency in adult men, and the body has no mechanism to excrete excess.

Iron overload — hereditary hemochromatosis — affects 1 in 250 people of Northern European descent. For those people, a daily multivitamin with iron is actively harmful over time. Liver damage. Diabetes. Joint problems. The label says "supports energy" and they don't know.

The rule we wrote "Never include an ingredient where the average consumer's default state is sufficiency and overdose has long-term consequences." Iron fails this test for men over 18. It can be essential for menstruating women — but that's a separate formula (Vesper Femme N° 03 · late 2026), not a universal one.

Instead, we include vitamin C with a meal, which doubles the body's own absorption of dietary iron from food. Net result: iron status improves if you were low, stays safe if you were high. This is what we mean by intelligent defaults.

E. Vázquez

Entry 05 · 20 January 2025

Omega form wars.

Dr. Elena Vázquez
Lead formulator

Two forms of omega-3 dominate the market. Most cheap fish-oil capsules use the ethyl ester form (EE). A few premium brands use the re-esterified triglyceride form (rTG). They are not interchangeable.

FormProcessingAbsorptionCost / g EPA+DHAStability
Ethyl ester (EE)Industrial distillation~50% slower$0.04Moderate
Re-esterified triglycerideRe-converted after purificationMatches body's native form$0.11High
Natural triglycerideUnpurifiedNative form$0.03Heavy metal contamination

We use rTG. It costs 3.7× more per gram. The cost is worth it because your body has no enzymes for ethyl esters — it has to break them apart first, which takes energy, produces ethanol as a byproduct, and reduces absorption by half. It's like buying a $500 wine and drinking it through a filter.

Separately: we rejected all unpurified natural-TG options because independent testing of 30 samples showed 19 had detectable PCBs above our internal ceiling. Cold Norwegian waters filtered through our supplier's 5-step molecular distillation was the only source that passed.

E. Vázquez

Entry 06 · 18 March 2025

Why the bottle holds two, not eight.

Dr. Elena Vázquez
Lead formulator

We had a choice. We could have put everything into one daily capsule, shrinking each active to the lowest effective dose — the formulation most competitors take to be "convenient." Or we could have done eight capsules at maximally-studied doses. We chose two. Here's why.

Mathematically, 600mg of KSM-66 ashwagandha (our chosen dose) is already too much mass for a single size-0 capsule, even before you add anything else. Two capsules let us deliver: 400mg elemental magnesium, 600mg KSM-66, 500mg curcumin, 1g omega-3 and nine other actives at their effective doses.

The hardest trade-off We sacrificed the one-capsule marketing story. We kept the doses that work. If a customer can't manage two capsules, they can't manage the supplement habit at all — and that's worth being honest about. Correct dose is not a convenience feature.

We also considered splitting it across an AM and PM capsule — aligning stimulating actives (B-complex, ashwagandha in the morning) with calming ones (magnesium, glycine in the evening). In user testing, compliance dropped 23% when people had to remember two times. Single-dose timing, two capsules, won.
